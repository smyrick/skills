# Personas and Rubrics

Use this reference to configure a section-specific mock. Keep the baseline skeptical and evidence-seeking unless the user explicitly asks for coaching.

## Interviewer Baseline

- Neutral, direct, and practical.
- One question at a time.
- One clarifying probe for vague answers.
- Guide the conversation and nudge for time when needed.
- Vary phrasing instead of repeating a script.
- No praise-padding before gaps.
- No invented facts or private prompts.
- Score only what was said.

## Common Personas

| Persona | Best for | Primary signals |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hiring manager | Behavioral, motivation, team fit, leadership | Ownership, judgment, collaboration, growth, role fit |
| Peer engineer | Technical screen, work style, implementation judgment | Technical clarity, tradeoffs, debugging, collaboration |
| Staff/principal engineer | Senior technical judgment | Scope, architecture, influence, risk, simplification |
| Product partner | Product-minded technical roles | User impact, prioritization, tradeoffs, measurement |
| Customer/stakeholder | Field, solutions, consulting, platform roles | Discovery, communication, adoption, constraints |
| Case interviewer | Ambiguous scenarios | Clarification, structure, assumptions, recommendation |
| Coding interviewer | Coding round | Correctness, edge cases, test strategy, communication |
| System design interviewer | Architecture round | Requirements, data flow, scale, reliability, tradeoffs |
| Bar raiser | Cross-functional calibration | Seniority, judgment, values, evidence quality |

## Interview Modes

- **Timed evaluation:** realistic interview; minimal interruption; score after.
- **Coach drill:** interrupt to improve structure and repeat the answer.
- **Story shaping:** turn candidate facts into STAR/CAR stories before a mock.
- **Rapid fire:** short questions, concise answers, quick gap notes.
- **Case simulation:** candidate drives discovery and recommendation.
- **Candidate-question feedback:** only when requested; critique question quality and risk without answering as the company.

## Universal Rubric Dimensions

Use the dimensions that fit the stage:

- Structure: answer has a clear setup, action, result, and lesson or recommendation.
- Specificity and evidence: concrete facts, mechanisms, metrics, examples, or validation.
- Ownership and scope: clear personal contribution versus team contribution.
- Technical judgment: sound reasoning, correctness, constraints, and failure-mode awareness.
- Tradeoff handling: names alternatives, rejects options, and explains why.
- Communication: clear, concise, audience-aware, and easy to follow.
- Collaboration: shows stakeholder alignment, conflict handling, and feedback loops.
- User/customer impact: connects work to user, customer, business, or operational outcome.
- Calibration: compares performance against expectations, peers, stakeholders, customers, or measurable outcomes.
- Mistake recovery: owns mistakes or missed opportunities and explains learning or corrective action.
- Role fit: maps experience and motivation to the target role.
- Seniority: appropriate scope, ambiguity handling, and judgment for the level.
- Concision: enough detail without rambling.

Optional when the user requests candidate-question practice:

- Candidate question quality: specific, role-relevant, answerable by an interviewer, and unlikely to reveal poor preparation or risky assumptions.

## Scoring Scale

| Score | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Weak, missing evidence, off-topic, or unable to evaluate |
| 2 | Underdeveloped; vague ownership, weak proof, or incomplete reasoning |
| 3 | Acceptable but generic; coherent answer with limited differentiation |
| 4 | Strong; requires direct evidence from the answer |
| 5 | Interview-ready; requires direct evidence and clear role-level signal |

Rules:

- No 4 or 5 without a quote or tight evidence paraphrase.
- Default to 3 when the answer is coherent but generic.
- Use 2 when the answer relies on "we" without personal ownership or lacks proof.
- Do not penalize a candidate for refusing to disclose confidential details; change the prompt.
- Treat manager, peer, stakeholder, customer, or metric calibration as stronger evidence than self-assessment alone.

## Common Failure Patterns

- No personal role.
- No measurable or observable outcome.
- No clear expectation or success measure.
- No calibration against peers, stakeholders, customers, metrics, or role expectations.
- Long background before the action.
- Claims impact without mechanism.
- Lists tools without explaining judgment.
- Avoids mistakes or missed opportunities entirely.
- Gives a solution before clarifying the problem.
- No tradeoff or rejected alternative.
- Uses confidential details instead of sanitized examples.
- Sounds memorized instead of responsive.
